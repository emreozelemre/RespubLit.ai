## Operational benchmark design and test protocol (50-item batch)

The goal of this benchmark was operational validation. We wanted to see whether the reviewer-matching pipeline already produces actionable, campaign-ready shortlists under low-information and noisy input conditions, which are common in real evaluation workflows. This is not a ground-truth accuracy study, because there is no authoritative “correct” reviewer list for each case. It is a readiness test for immediate use as a decision-support and shortlisting layer.

We did not use real grant proposals. Instead, we constructed a batch of 50 scientific papers randomly scraped from the OpenAlex database and treated them as mock proposals. Papers are often harder than proposals for reviewer discovery because they tend to be more focused and technically narrow. If the pipeline can retrieve plausible experts from publication-style abstracts, it should generalize well to broader proposal narratives. The sampling rule was deliberately simple. Each paper had at least five authors, and the batch was designed to cover a diverse range of domains, including interdisciplinary cases.

To keep the test realistic and demanding, we ran the pipeline in a deliberately low-information regime. For each item we provided only the title, abstract, applicants or authors (represented in applicant-like fields), and the reference list. We intentionally omitted the full body text. This was done to show that even when the main research narrative is unavailable, the pipeline can still infer meaningful concepts and recover strong candidates.

We also introduced an adversarial element by deliberately misnaming some documents. For example, a humanities item might be saved under a filename suggesting “environmental” content. The purpose was to ensure the system does not perform well only because of superficial cues, and that concept inference and reviewer retrieval are driven by the actual textual and bibliographic content rather than file naming artifacts.

The pipeline was executed across all 50 items and produced a ranked list of reviewer candidates per item, targeting up to roughly 60 candidates each. We then assessed the outputs the way a Science Officer would assess a real campaign shortlist generator, focusing on systemic usability rather than individual case-by-case content. We checked that every item had enough depth to support a top-20 working shortlist plus reserves, that list sizes were consistently large enough to survive invitation waves and declines, that ranks were contiguous and immediately usable, that there were no duplicate reviewers within a single list, and that reviewer reuse across the batch remained low enough to avoid bottlenecks in parallel campaign execution.

The batch-level results support an operationally optimistic conclusion. Every item had at least 22 reviewers, which means every case supports a complete top-20 shortlist with reserve capacity. The lists were consistently deep, with an average list size of 56.86 and a median of 59, and 45 out of 50 items contained 50 or more reviewers. Ranking was clean and contiguous from 1 to N in every file, so the output can be used directly as top-10 and top-20 working shortlists without repair or reformatting. There were no duplicate reviewers within any single item list.

Reviewer concentration across the batch was also very low, which matters for real campaign execution. Across the 500 top-10 slots there were 492 unique reviewers, and across the 1,000 top-20 slots there were 981 unique reviewers, with no reviewer appearing more than twice in the top-20 across the full batch. This indicates the system is not over-recycling the same names and is less likely to create capacity bottlenecks when many evaluations run in parallel.

Overall, the system is ready now as an operational reviewer discovery and shortlisting layer. It is robust enough to support decision-making, it is clearly usable for shortlisting, and it is suitable for campaign initiation as long as final invitations remain lightly supervised. This officer-led framing is not a concession to weak lists. It reflects how these outputs are most effectively used in practice. The top of each list is strong enough to drive immediate invitation waves, while the lower-ranked portion is best treated as reserve depth for declines and substitutions rather than an automatic invitation queue. The pipeline accelerates and prioritizes the workflow, but it should sit inside the normal Science Officer process rather than replace final invitation judgment.

For data privacy reasons, the released benchmark output has been sanitized. Author names, reviewer names, and other property-engineering fields have been removed. Reviewer OpenAlex identifiers remain present so that third parties can still inspect and test the output quality through reproducible metadata lookups and auditing workflows without exposing personal data.
